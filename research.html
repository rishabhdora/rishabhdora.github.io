Light-Matter Interactions & Quantum Transport

My research sits at the intersection of electromagnetic theory, quantum dynamics, and high-performance computing, with the goal of understanding how laser fields drive charge dynamics in molecular and nanoscale junctions.

Graduate Research · University of Rochester

Light-Matter Interactions Beyond the Dipole Approximation

The dipole approximation is the standard starting point for describing how light couples to matter, but it breaks down for situations when the spot size of light is finite, at interfaces, or in complex electromagnetic environments. We have developed a general theoretical and computational framework for modeling light-matter interactions in extended systems beyond the dipole approximation. We employ this framework to quantify the limitations of the ubiquitous dipole approximation and to overcome well-known issues associated with multipolar expansions.

Light-matter interactions Beyond Dipole Electromagnetic Simulations
Graduate Research · University of Rochester

Time-Dependent Quantum Transport

We implemented nonequilibrium Green's function (NEGF) methods for time-dependent quantum transport. The framework is applied to study laser-driven charge dynamics in atomistic junctions, extracting physically meaningful observables relevant to light-field control and emergent phenomena in materials.

NEGF Quantum Transport Laser-Driven Dynamics
Graduate Research · University of Rochester

Control of Real and Virtual Charge Carriers using ω + 2ω Pulses

Using a two-color (ω + 2ω) laser pulse scheme, we study the coherent control of real and virtual charge carriers in gold-graphene-gold heterojunction. This work explores how the relative phase and amplitude of the two-color pulse components can be used to selectively drive and control charge dynamics on ultrafast timescales.

Coherent Control Two-Color Pulses
MS Thesis Research · IISER Bhopal · 2021-2022

Polaron Transport in Optomechanical Arrays

For my MS thesis, we investigated polaron transport in optomechanical arrays as a platform for quantum simulations. We developed model Hamiltonians for electron-phonon coupling, and performed numerical simulations to study charge localization, transport, and many-body effects.

Binary Star Fractions in Open Star Clusters

As an IASc-INSA-NASI Summer Research Fellow, we performed large-scale astrometric data analysis using Gaia DR2 data to study the fraction of binary star systems in open star clusters. We analyzed stellar proper motions using statistical filtering techniques to separate cluster members from field stars and identify binary candidates.
